\begin{notebox}
\textbf{Major planning update in this revision.} Module 1 remains the Geant4 radiation-deposition module, while \textbf{Modules 2 through 6 are now planned as two-dimensional models}. One-dimensional checks may still be used privately as verification subproblems, but the official module definitions and interfaces below are now \textbf{2D-first}.
\end{notebox}

\section{Project objective}
The project title is \textbf{Radiation-Induced Defect Evolution and Device Degradation in Silicon}. The central question is how a microscopic radiation interaction inside silicon becomes a macroscopic change in device behavior.

The physics chain is:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item incident radiation deposits energy in silicon,
    \item deposited energy seeds point defects and defect complexes,
    \item those defects migrate, react, cluster, and anneal,
    \item charged defects alter the internal electrostatic landscape,
    \item the changed field and trap population alter carrier transport,
    \item thermal evolution modifies rates and mobilities,
    \item the coupled system produces measurable device degradation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Module roadmap}
\begin{longtable}{p{0.06\linewidth} p{0.31\linewidth} p{0.55\linewidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{No.} & \textbf{Module} & \textbf{Purpose}\\
\midrule
1 & Geant4 radiation energy deposition in silicon & Compute where, when, and how much energy is deposited in silicon by the incident radiation field. Export a deposition map that seeds later material-damage or defect-generation models.\\
2 & Two-dimensional electrostatics with defect-dependent space charge & Solve the two-dimensional electrostatic problem for a damaged silicon structure. Convert dopants and charged defects into electric potential, electric field, and depletion-structure changes.\\
3 & Two-dimensional defect evolution: diffusion, reaction, migration, and annealing & Evolve vacancies, interstitials, complexes, and effective defect species in two spatial dimensions as functions of time and temperature.\\
4 & Two-dimensional thermal transport coupled to defect evolution & Solve the temperature field in the device and use it to update diffusion coefficients, reaction rates, annealing rates, and other temperature-dependent physics.\\
5 & Two-dimensional drift-diffusion carrier transport with defect-assisted recombination & Predict how the damaged device transports electrons and holes under the altered field and trap landscape.\\
6 & Coupled two-dimensional multiphysics degradation model & Couple Modules 2--5 into a self-consistent simulator for defect evolution, field evolution, thermal evolution, and carrier-transport degradation.\\
7 & Multiscale extrapolation and scalable prediction & Develop methods for making predictions on much larger spatial, temporal, or statistical scales than the direct high-fidelity simulation can afford. This module covers controlled approximations, quantified error bars, and statistical upscaling.\\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}

\section{Core architecture decision: 2D for Modules 2--6}
\subsection{Why the move to 2D matters}
The original one-dimensional path was numerically attractive, but it suppresses several effects that become important once the damage pattern is spatially structured:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item lateral spreading of defect populations,
    \item lateral field bending and edge effects,
    \item nonuniform temperature fields near localized energy deposition,
    \item current crowding and local carrier-flow rerouting,
    \item spatial mismatch between Geant4 deposition structure and downstream device solvers.
\end{itemize}

A two-dimensional architecture is therefore the more faithful bridge between a realistic Geant4 source pattern and later electrostatic, thermal, and transport calculations.

\subsection{Practical interpretation}
``2D'' here means the main solvers are planned on an \((x,y)\) mesh. For time-dependent modules the fields become functions such as
\[
C(x,y,t), \qquad \phi(x,y,t), \qquad T(x,y,t), \qquad n(x,y,t), \qquad p(x,y,t).
\]
Module 1 can still export either a true 2D deposition map directly or a set of event/deposition products that are binned into a 2D source map before passing to MATLAB.

\subsection{What still stays simple}
The move to 2D does \emph{not} mean every solver should start maximally complex. The first versions should still keep:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item simple rectangular domains,
    \item structured grids,
    \item small defect networks,
    \item simple boundary conditions,
    \item weak one-way coupling before self-consistent feedback.
\end{itemize}

\section{Recommended coding order}
The most stable implementation order is still physics-driven rather than strictly numerical:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.6em]
    \item \textbf{Module 1:} Geant4 geometry, scoring, and export format.
    \item \textbf{Module 3:} two-dimensional defect evolution on a structured grid.
    \item \textbf{Module 2:} two-dimensional electrostatics with prescribed charged defects.
    \item \textbf{Module 4:} two-dimensional thermal solver plus temperature-dependent coefficients.
    \item \textbf{Module 5:} two-dimensional drift-diffusion with prescribed electrostatic field and defect/trap fields.
    \item \textbf{Module 6:} weak coupling, then stronger feedback and iterative convergence.
    \item \textbf{Module 7:} scalable-prediction methods after at least one credible high-fidelity coupled workflow exists.
\end{enumerate}

This order starts with the damage-state evolution because later modules require the defect field. Electrostatics should be debugged before full carrier transport. Module 7 should come \emph{after} at least one trustworthy baseline exists, because any approximation or scaling strategy needs a reference solution against which its error can be measured.

\section{Module interfaces in the new 2D plan}
\subsection{Module 1 to Module 3 handoff}
The first strong interface should now be a \textbf{2D deposition or defect-source map}. A minimal handoff object should contain:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \path{x}: one-dimensional coordinate array in meters,
    \item \path{y}: one-dimensional coordinate array in meters,
    \item \path{Edep2D}: deposited-energy density field in \si{J.m^{-3}},
    \item optional \path{tBins} and time-resolved source information,
    \item metadata describing bin width, device geometry, radiation case, and units.
\end{itemize}

A clean first file target is
\begin{center}
\path{matlab/input/module1_to_module3_2d.mat}
\end{center}
so that the MATLAB side is not forced to parse raw Geant4 text every time.

\subsection{Module 3 to Module 2 handoff}
After a defect-evolution run, MATLAB should save
\begin{center}
\path{matlab/output/module3_to_module2_2d.mat}
\end{center}
containing at minimum:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item \path{x}, \path{y},
    \item \path{Cfinal(:,:,i)} for defect species or effective defect fields,
    \item \path{chargedDefectDensity2D} in \si{m^{-3}},
    \item \path{trapDensity2D} for later transport use,
    \item time-history diagnostics for provenance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Module 2 and Module 4 to Module 5 handoff}
Carrier transport should read:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item electric potential \(\phi(x,y)\),
    \item electric field components \(E_x(x,y), E_y(x,y)\),
    \item temperature \(T(x,y)\),
    \item trap or recombination parameters derived from the defect state.
\end{itemize}

\section{Verification strategy under the 2D architecture}
The jump to 2D increases realism, but it also increases the number of ways a solver can fail. Each module therefore needs at least one low-complexity verification path.

\begin{longtable}{p{0.16\linewidth} p{0.75\linewidth}}
\toprule
\textbf{Module} & \textbf{Verification logic}\\
\midrule
1 & Compare deposition trends against known silicon behavior and verify that the exported 2D binning is internally consistent.\\
2 & Use manufactured or symmetric charge configurations whose 2D potential and field patterns are physically obvious; verify sign conventions and boundary-condition behavior.\\
3 & Check pure diffusion, pure annealing, and symmetry-preserving source cases in 2D. Total inventory should behave correctly under the chosen boundaries.\\
4 & Recover known 2D heat-conduction behavior on simple rectangular domains with simple source terms and boundary conditions.\\
5 & Verify defect-free transport first, then turn on simple trap-assisted recombination. Check that zero-field or symmetric cases behave sensibly.\\
6 & Confirm that decoupled or weakly coupled limits reduce to standalone solutions. Track iterative residuals and relaxation behavior.\\
7 & Every scalable-prediction method must be benchmarked against a higher-fidelity reference. The benchmark should report both speedup and error metrics.\\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}

\section{Module 7: multiscale extrapolation and scalable prediction}
\subsection{Why Module 7 is needed}
A direct two-dimensional coupled simulation can become expensive when one tries to predict:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item much larger device areas,
    \item much longer annealing times,
    \item many radiation realizations,
    \item rare-event or tail-probability behavior,
    \item large parameter sweeps over temperature, bias, dose, or geometry.
\end{itemize}

That is the motivation for a distinct roadmap item focused on scaling the predictive capability, not merely speeding up one code path.

\subsection{Recommended name}
A stronger title than ``scaling up methods of the simulation'' is:
\begin{center}
\textbf{Module 7. Multiscale Extrapolation and Scalable Prediction}
\end{center}
This name is better because it covers both controlled approximation and statistical scaling, without implying that the only objective is raw performance.

\subsection{Option A: controlled reduced-fidelity approximation}
This branch intentionally simplifies the physics or numerics to gain speed. Examples include:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item coarser spatial meshes,
    \item simplified defect networks,
    \item operator splitting that neglects weak feedback terms in some iterations,
    \item quasi-steady assumptions for the fastest-relaxing field,
    \item reduced-order models built from high-fidelity snapshots,
    \item approximate Green-function or homogenized closures for selected subproblems.
\end{itemize}

The key requirement is not merely to run faster, but to \textbf{quantify what fidelity is lost}. For that reason, Module 7 should define an error metric relative to a higher-fidelity reference.

\subsubsection{Suggested error-bar framework}
For any approximate model, report at least:
\[
\varepsilon_Q = \frac{\lVert Q_{\mathrm{approx}} - Q_{\mathrm{ref}} \rVert}{\lVert Q_{\mathrm{ref}} \rVert},
\]
where \(Q\) is a chosen quantity of interest, such as peak defect density, leakage current, charge-collection efficiency, depletion width, or a spatial field map. In addition, report a \textbf{speedup factor}
\[
S = \frac{t_{\mathrm{ref}}}{t_{\mathrm{approx}}}.
\]
A useful project-level trade plot is then \(\varepsilon_Q\) versus \(S\), rather than speed alone.

For spatial fields one may also use weighted metrics such as
\[
\varepsilon_{w} = \frac{\left(\int w(\vect{r})\,|Q_{\mathrm{approx}}(\vect{r}) - Q_{\mathrm{ref}}(\vect{r})|^2\,\dd A\right)^{1/2}}{\left(\int w(\vect{r})\,|Q_{\mathrm{ref}}(\vect{r})|^2\,\dd A\right)^{1/2}},
\]
where the weight \(w(\vect{r})\) emphasizes physically important regions such as junctions, depletion edges, or high-field regions.

\subsection{Option B: statistical approximation and upscaling}
This branch keeps the local physics model but uses statistics to predict larger-scale behavior. Examples include:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item using many smaller simulated tiles to infer a larger effective device response,
    \item propagating event statistics rather than simulating every event explicitly,
    \item surrogate distributions for defect-source fields,
    \item rare-event estimators or importance-weighted sampling,
    \item uncertainty propagation from source statistics to device-level degradation metrics.
\end{itemize}

This is especially relevant when the true goal is a prediction for a much larger area, a longer exposure, or a population-level reliability statement.

\subsection{A third path worth keeping in view}
There is a third useful category beyond the two branches above:
\begin{center}
\textbf{Hybrid multiresolution coupling}
\end{center}
In this approach, only the physically critical region is simulated at high fidelity, while surrounding regions are treated with a cheaper closure or coarser model. Examples include local mesh refinement, embedded fine-grid windows around damage hot spots, or domain decomposition in which only one subregion uses the full coupled physics.

This third category often preserves more local fidelity than a globally reduced model, while still reducing cost significantly.
